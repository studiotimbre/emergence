Music’s potency to communicate feelings and distill memories captivates Kimberly Hunt. Through the piano, she reveals the intricate design and depth of story in piano masterworks and tells her own stories through composition. Kimberly studies with Phillip Silver – a journey that constantly deepens her appreciation of the art of piano.

With sound, Kimberly paints imagery and expands emotions of her pianistic journey. "emergence" is a celebration of this creative journey – a story of commitment, endeavor, reflection and reaching for new horizons. This story is ongoing. “emergence” will continue to evolve. Thank you for sharing this journey.

Andante spianato et grande polonaise brillante

Frédéric Chopin

Political turmoil in Poland separated Chopin from his homeland, a rift that left him yearning for peace. Taking refuge in Paris, Chopin developed his individual and poetic pianistic voice in composition and performance. In the summer of 1836, an opportunity arose to visit his family in Poland, and shortly after he proposed marriage to a young Polish lady. Over the following year, as Chopin waited hopefully for an answer, this composition was published.

The introductory andante spianato is a shimmering poem for solo piano. Its melodic line unravels effortlessly, now and then dissolving into a birdsong like cascade.

The ensuing polonaise is hopeful and bold with drama and courage. Originally written for piano and orchestra, the fabric of the polonaise is an interplay of contrasting gestures that channel each other onwards. The orchestra opens in a deft sweeping gesture. The piano responds, soaring into a bright, confident, and colorful melody. Interweaving with the orchestra, the piano melody rises in floridity and breadth, until a sudden outburst of angular dramatic gestures fall into the central lyrical theme. This lyrical theme begins in a deep misty atmosphere, then expands in immediacy and intensity until a brighter tone returns and leads the piece towards a courageous heroic coda.

Piano Sonata No. 23 in F Minor "Appassionata"

Ludwig van Beethoven

Appassionata is a tightly knit masterwork of overwhelming depth and drama, reaching beyond the tones of Beethoven’s day.

I. Allegro assai: In the opening movement, every gesture is propelled by urgency and momentum. Notes are sharpened with immense emotional force. A rapid and intent pulse acts as a driving undercurrent, unifying the bold and varied gestures. The harmonies lift to dramatic peaks that Beethoven then dashes into stark moments of crisis from which an enduring hero emerges.

II. Andante con moto: The andante opens like a deep storm cloud bearing over the countryside with grandeur and majesty. Yet, there is something sad within, creating a sense of ominous serenity. Slowly, gradually, the somber density breaks up, fostering a little spark of hope and transforming the heavy emotion into a moment of springtime and sunshine. The theme returns as a duet. At the brink of the ending, at the very note of resolution, the duet does not answer. Instead a mysterious, lonely and empty color sounds, followed in a split second by a piercing chord that shatters hope.

III. Allegro ma non troppo: Any sense of springtime is chilled and swept away. Thin suspenseful lines whisk about like a scurrying wind, building towards flashes of lightning and a vortex of dramatic impassioned statements. Daringly quiet and mysterious transitions sharpen our attention, offering glimpses into the character of the hero.

Étude-tableau Op. 39 No. 2 & No. 3

Sergei Rachmaninoff

Rachmaninoff's Étude-tableaux or "study pictures" are vivid, potent, and artistically crafted miniatures composed during 1916-17. These were Rachmaninoff's final years in Russia – a time shadowed by rising revolutionary tensions.

No. 2 • Lento assai: A lucid yet dense atmosphere pervades this etude, like the damp charge in the air before a storm. Elements of its contour and atmosphere are eerily reminiscent of Rachmaninoff's symphonic tone poem, the Isle of the Dead. This piano tone poem opens with a pearly fabric, like the reflections of a morning sky on still water. This fabric is woven of small melodic ideas that interlock into extraordinarily potent harmonies. At first, the harmonies lift the texture skyward before plummeting into a dense and ominous inner sequence. Here the fabric of the piece condenses and swells with the massive weight of ocean waves, before thinning into transparency once more.

No. 3 • Allegro molto: Rhythmic vitality propels this etude onward, like a sleigh dashing through a dramatic landscape. Rachmaninoff transforms almost identical rhythmic ideas with skillful and explorative harmonic coloring, creating contrasting moments of tearful lyricism or the exhilarating rush of a soaring updraft. In closing, the melody spins down in transparent threads, lengthening and deepening like shadows on a chill winter night.

Gaspard de la Nuit I. Ondine

Maurice Ravel

Gaspard de la Nuit ("treasurer of the night") is Ravel's daring rendering of three poems by Aloysius Bertrand. The first, Ondine, is a fairytale of a water nymph. Ravel does not shy away from demanding pianistic figurations in his pursuit for unexpected and ingenious contours and colors.

This surreal rendering of water captures the magic of water in motion. Fluid gestures give the impression of sweeping waves, and delicate notes evoke droplets dappling back into the surface.

A small, softly sung melody is interwoven like a fine thread of pearls swirling in the water. Sequence after sequence rises then cadences into mystery and depth. The rippling texture shimmers with an aura of wonder, creating a spellbound effect like a mirage.
